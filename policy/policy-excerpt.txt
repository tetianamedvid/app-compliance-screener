It’s important to use your best judgment in terms of how the items are being displayed, how services are marketed or labeled, and if the merchant uses innuendos to suggest products or services other than those explicitly advertised. Content Creation 
(High Risk - Not Enabled for Wix)


Guideline 
This category is considered high-risk and is not available for Wix at this time.

Why are Content Creator Platforms risky? 
Content creator platforms are considered high-risk businesses due to the potential for triggering  regulatory thresholds related to money transmission which vary across countries and sub jurisdictions.  In addition, Stripe’s limited appetite to conduct content monitoring and/or enforce content moderation  on third party platforms (e.g. Twitter, Tik Tok) increases the risk that any monetized content may  violate Stripe’s prohibited business lists.  

What are Content Creator Platforms? 
Content creation platforms (“platforms”) are those that connect the platform’s user base to content  created by other users (“creators”). To enable platforms and their creators to monetize content, Stripe  currently supports payment processing for US-based platforms and their creators, who may be based  in the US or any of the countries for which Stripe supports cross-border payouts. Stripe classifies  payments through content creation platforms along the following four categories: (1) purchase of  access to exclusive content (2) Purchase of digital goods, (3) Content-related tips, and (4) Money  exchanged without an underlying piece of unique digital content. In addition to these categories, Stripe  restrictions and prohibitions on peer to peer money transfers and unsupportable categories apply to all  content creator platforms.  

Subcategory 1: Purchase of access to exclusive content 
Transactions that enable end-users to purchase access to exclusive content, such as access to a live  digital event, single piece of content (e.g. image or video), or subscriber-only feed.

Transactions that enable end-users to receive digital goods, such as badges or coins that give the  purchaser special privileges or recognition. This does not include the sale or purchase of Non-Fungible  Tokens (NFTs), which are subject to Stripe’s separate NFT Policy and must be onboarded via the  Sparta review process.  

Not Enabled for Wix

Subcategory 3: Content-related tips 
Transactions that enable an end-user to provide compensation to a creator while viewing content (e. g. a tip by an end-user to a creator during a live event, while viewing a post, or while previewing  content in a profile, directory, or catalog under specific conditions).  

The ability for an end-user to tip a creator must be subsequent or simultaneous (but not  prior) to consumption of content in the product’s UI; tipping before any content is accessed by  the payor is unsupportable (e.g., the tip button exists on the creator’s profile page that does  not include any content). Tipping while viewing mere previews of content in a profile, directory, or  catalog, may be supportable; however, at minimum, the previews of the content must include the core  elements of the underlying content (e.g. the preview cannot be a still photo if the underlying content is  meant to be audio-focused, such as in a sing-along app).

(High Risk - Not Enabled for Wix)

Guideline

Why is this category risky? 
Our financial partners restrict Cryptocurrency Products and Services as they pose an increased credit risk and risk for money laundering. Regulation of the industry varies highly from region to region and the  industry continues to rapidly change. 

What are Cryptocurrency Products and Services? 

Cryptocurrency: A cryptocurrency is a digital asset designed to work as a medium of exchange that  uses strong cryptography to secure financial transactions, control the creation of additional units, and  verify the transfer of assets. Cryptocurrencies are a kind of alternative currency and digital currency. 

There are different types of cryptocurrencies. The most common types include:  
Digital Payment Tokens and utility tokens (unbacked tokens, such as BTC and ETH) Stablecoins (either asset- or algorithm-backed) and e-money tokens (E.g., United Stated digital  coin) 
Privacy Coins are designed to keep user transactions anonymous leaving no trace of how the  money moved through the network(E.g., DASH, Monero)  
Commodity tokens is backed by specific, pre-existing assets 
Security tokens are digital assets that represent traditional “securities.” Some security tokens  represent company shares or bonds which they issue to raise funds from investors. 

Cryptocurrency Exchange: Most commonly, exchanges enable their end-users to convert fiat  currencies, such as US dollars, to cryptocurrencies or to trade crypto currencies. This is done through  trading platforms and other products like cryptocurrency wallets with extended functionalities. 

Cryptocurrency Wallet: This can be either a digital or hardware wallet that allows its owner to store  cryptocurrency or tokens. Some wallets also enable its owners to buy, sell, swap or stake their  cryptocurrency or tokens.  

Cryptocurrency Mining: Mining in this context refers to a process in which networks of specialized  computers verify and secure blockchains – the virtual ledgers that document cryptocurrency transactions.  In return for the processing power provided, the owners of the mining computers receive a reward -  typically units of the cryptocurrency they helped verify and secure.  

Initial Coin Offering (ICO): A process in which investors can buy into a crypto-based project through  newly issued cryptocurrency tokens. ICO participants are gambling that a currently worthless currency  will later increase in value above its original purchase price. 

Cryptocurrency Staking: A process in which owners of cryptocurrency can deposit their coins to  participate in running the blockchain it's built on and maintaining its security. In exchange, owners of a  cryptocurrency earn rewards calculated in percentage yield. This is possible only for cryptocurrencies  that use the “proof-of-stake” method to guarantee cryptocurrency's integrity.  

Cryptocurrency Lending: A process in which borrowers use digital assets as collateral for loans. These  loans are typically offered through a specialized platform. 

Cryptocurrency Yield Accounts: Offering where the owner of cryptocurrency agrees to lend out their  crypto assets in exchange for interest. This is similar to how savings accounts work at banks: You  deposit money, then the bank lends it out and pays you back plus interest.

Virtual world credits, which are covered under our Stored Value policy. 
Non-fungible Tokens (NFTs), which are addressed in the Non-Fungible Tokens policy.

Debt and Credit Services

Guideline
Stripe prohibits agencies or firms involved in the collection of past debt or past due receivables, debt reduction, debt settlement, debt relief, debt adjusting, or debt consolidation services. Stripe prohibits the sale of credit repair and credit monitoring services, and restricts the sale of credit reports. Stripe places restrictions on agencies or firms involved in the collection of past debt or past due receivables.
Why are Debt and Credit Services risky?
Debt and credit related services pose risks of predatory practices. Federal law, Federal agencies (of note, Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)) state that practices that use false, deceptive, misleading, or abusive practice are prohibited. Additionally, our financial partners list collection agencies, debt relief service providers, and credit services as prohibited.

Subcategory 1: Debt Collection Services
Debt collectors are a person or a company that regularly collects debts or past due receivables owed to others. There are also called debt collection agencies, debt collection companies, or debt buyers. 
Debt collectors buy past-due debts from creditors or other businesses and then try to collect payment of the debt from the debtor.
Lawyers and law firms who collect debt as part of their practice are considered debt collectors.
A user is also considered to be collecting debt if they use Stripe to accept credit card payments for repayment of debt, this creates new debt.
Note: This subcategory does not include merchants who provide a SaaS platform for the repayment of debt or bills, so long as the platform is only a technology service and does not negotiate debt.

Not Supportable
Regional Summary 
This subcategory is not supportable in all jurisdictions.



Subcategory 2: Debt Relief Companies

Debt relief or debt settlement companies claim to renegotiate, settle, or in some way change the terms of a person’s debt to a creditor or debt collector. While debt relief companies often charge expensive fees, they typically cannot negotiate better terms with creditors than the debtor. 
Companies that say they can renegotiate, settle, or in some way change the terms of a person's debt to a creditor or debt collector. These companies tend to promise certain results. They can be called various names:
Debt relief
Debt settlement
Debt consolidation
Debt reduction
Debt adjusting
Companies that offer debt protection

Behaviors for debt relief companies:
They charge fees in exchange for the service of settling debts.
They state they can settle all of you debt for a promised percentage reduction.
They tout "new government program" to bail out personal credit card debt.
They guarantee they can make debt go away.
They advise to stop communicating with your creditors.
They claim they can stop all debt collection calls and lawsuits.
They guarantee that unsecured debts can be paid off at a significantly reduced price.

Examples of marketing phrases for debt relief companies:
“Wipe out your debts! Consolidate your bills! How? By using the protection and assistance provided by federal law. For once, let the law work for you!"
“Consolidate your bills into one monthly payment without borrowing.”
“STOP credit harassment, foreclosures, repossessions, tax levies, and garnishments.”
“Keep Your Property.”

Note: This subcategory does not include merchants who provide a SaaS platform for the repayment of debt or bills, so long as the platform is only a technology service and does not negotiate debt.

Not Supportable
Regional Summary 
This subcategory is not supportable in all jurisdictions.


Subcategory 3: Credit Repair Services

A credit repair service is a for-profit entity that charges a fee to improve a consumer’s credit score through a remediation effort. Credit repair could include investigating and disputing items on one’s credit report. 

Note:  Credit building is not credit repair. Credit building is where a customer makes regular payments on debt, which the service provider reports to credit bureaus (E.g. Experian). While this is not credit repair, some credit building services may involve lending (E.g. lines of credit). See the Financial Services section of the SHG that covers this topic if lending services are involved.

Fundraising

Guideline
Fundraising is restricted on Stripe. Fundraising encompasses several types of users, including charities, crowdfunders, political organizations, other social welfare and fraternal organizations, among others. Fundraising may be permissible when certain criteria and requirements are met. Stripe prohibits certain user types and jurisdictions pursuant to financial partner expectations and local regulatory requirements.
Why is Fundraising risky?
Users who are fundraising are generally considered high-risk business for a number of reasons, including increased regulatory focus, risk of prohibited money transmission transactions, inherent risk of financial crime, financial partner risk, and fraud risk. 

Note: All crowdfunding users and certain charity and non-profit users are subject to supportability reviews and Enhanced Due Diligence. 
What is Fundraising?
Fundraising refers to the act of raising money from numerous individuals for a specific cause, charity, project, goal, or business. Fundraising can refer to charities, political organizations, charitable gambling, crowdfunders, or other business operations soliciting donations. 

A registered charity is registered with a government entity as soliciting donations for a charitable cause. An organization may be soliciting donations for and serving a charitable purpose without being considered a registered charity. This type of user may fall into any number of categories, including crowdfunding for a charitable purpose, political organizations, membership organizations, and social welfare organizations. Both registered charities and organizations soliciting donations for a charitable purpose may be supportable on Stripe, but depending upon their user type and region/country may be subject to additional review (supportability check and Enhanced Due Diligence).

Fundraising users, particularly crowdfunding users, can either be standalone organizations soliciting funds, or platforms that provide the infrastructure for multiple campaigns to solicit donations from multiple donors. Crowdfunding has a number of different use cases, including:
Donations to registered charities
Donations for a charitable/personal cause
Funding the creation of a physical product that will be delivered at a later date
Donations to registered political parties.
What is a Charitable Purpose?
A charitable purpose refers to fundraising endeavors that serve a clearly defined and specific objective aimed at benefiting the betterment of society, like providing relief for the poor, distressed, or underprivileged, advancing education or science, promoting religion, and combating community deterioration and juvenile delinquency or

Fundraising endeavors that serve a clearly defined and specific objective aimed at benefiting the betterment of an individual or team/organization who demonstrates a clear intention to fulfill the specified cause or purpose for which donations are received.

Subcategory 1: Registered Charities

​​Users classified under this subcategory are government-registered charitable organizations. Supportable charities must typically meet the following criteria*: 
The charitable organization must be engaged in soliciting charitable donations and contributions for social good where the donor does not receive any goods or services in return for their donation.
The charitable organization does not attempt to influence legislation as a substantial part of its activities.
The charitable organization does not participate in any campaign activity for or against political candidates, or political fundraising (see Subcategory 3).
This subcategory includes NGOs.

*Note: In certain jurisdictions such as Thailand, both registered and unregistered charities are not supportable regardless of whether the criteria have been met.

* *Note: If a user falls into Subcategory 1, that designation supersedes any other subcategories.

Gambling and Games of Skill with Prizes of Monetary Value 

Guideline 
This section would provide an overview of our general supportability/unsupportability of the category, and  what it includes. 

Why is this category risky? 
Gambling is a highly regulated industry and its status ranges from being illegal to full regulated commercialized market. The complex regulatory environment and requirements for both the potential  users and for Stripe are the reason why this category is highly restricted.  

Gambling users are considered “high brand risk” by the card networks and require a specific high-risk  MCC assignment. Outside of the US, a standard HBR registration process is necessary to make  gambling users supportable. For US users, a dedicated flow that goes beyond standard HBR  requirements exists.  

A user's websites can host multiple types of gambling activities. With all types of gambling restricted on  Stripe, this will not make a difference to the outcome of the review. For the purpose of the review, either  the most prominent activity should be considered OR in the case of scaled review, the first activity that  will be confirmed through a question tree will be confirmed. 

Subcategory 1: Betting and Fantasy Sports 
Betting is the making or accepting a bet on any of the following: 
The outcome of a race, competition or other event or process. These can be real-world, simulated or  imaginary.  
The likelihood of anything occurring or not occurring 
Whether anything is or is not true.  

This policy applies both to users who use Stripe to accept bets as well as users who would use Stripe to  payout winnings of said bets. Examples of betting activity include but is not limited to:  
Stripe is used to accept a bet and/or payout a bet that would be placed on the outcome of the  real world, e-sport or a fantasy sports event/match.  
Stripe is used to accept a bet/and or payout a bet on the outcome of a horse or a greyhound  race.  
Stripe is used to accept a bet and/or payout a bet that would be placed on the outcome of non sport related events like elections, talent competition or other real-world events outcomes.  Stripe is used to accept a bet and/or payout a bet that would be placed on the outcome of a  fantasy sports league. 
Stripe is used to fund a wallet that is then used to place bets.  
Stripe is used in a B2B environment as a financial infrastructure that involves any proceeds  from betting or any other regulated or unregulated betting activity.  

Example of a sports bet: The betting company accepts bets on the outcome of a football match between  2 teams and sets odds that reflect the risk and reward associated with said bet. The bettor then places a  bet, and if successful in their prediction, they receive a payout of winnings in the amount relative to the  bet they placed & to the odds and other conditions of the bet. Examples of companies that accept online  sport bets are Ladbrokers or Paddypower 

What are fantasy sports?: For the purposes of this policy, fantasy sports are games where participants  compete against each other using statistics from real-world sport/e-sport competitions. There are  different formats of these games, but for the purposes of this policy, we are concerned with those that  allow the participants to wager bets on the outcome of the competition between two or more imaginary  sports teams/players.  

Particularly relevant are so-called “Fantasy daily sports” which are an accelerated variant of traditional  fantasy sports that are conducted over short-term periods, such as a week or single day of competition,  as opposed to those that are played across an entire season.  

Important note: In the majority of countries, there is no distinction between fantasy sports and sports betting. So while in some U.S. states, fantasy sports can be classified as games of skill, for the purpose  of this policy, fantasy sports should not be classified as such.  

Examples of companies that operate fantasy sports betting are Fanduel and DraftKings.  

Important note: If a user's website only refers visitors to other betting websites, it should not be  considered a betting site. 

Not Supportable

This subcategory is not supportable in all jurisdictions.

Casinos enable gamblers to play in, wager money on and win money in casino games.  

Examples of games played in casinos include, but are not limited to - blackjack, baccarat, poker, slot  machines, roulette, bingo, wheel of fortune, mahjong, pachinko, razzle-dazzle, sic bo etc.

Stripe is used to accept a wager so that a player can play a casino game and/or payout  winnings that would be paid out as the outcome of the game.  
Stripe is used to fund a wallet that is then used by the player to take part in casino games.  Stripe is used in a B2B environment as a financial infrastructure that involves any proceeds  from the gambling activity (e.g. profit from operating of the casino games)  

Example of an online casino is 888casino.com. Example of a brick-and-mortar casino is Ceasar's in Las Vegas.

Subcategory 4: Other games of chance 

Definition: Any game that has three observable elements - chance, consideration (e.g purchase of a  ticket) and a prize of value - and that is not covered under any other subcategory within this policy.  Examples include, but are not limited to:

Box-breaking - box or case of cards is divided up among collectors who purchase the right to  receive cards as part of a random drawing and the contents are not identified. Can be also  referred to as a “group break”. User is supportable on Stripe while offering box breaking if:

Mystery boxes are part of a broader merchandise offering of a store. (e.g mystery box selling last-season clothes on a clothing store website). OR

Mystery boxes sold as a way to offer a combination of surprise physical goods every month (e.g monthly random 6 beer subscription).

Contents of the digital mystery/loot box are identified. This means that it is possible for the buyer to know what items that can be obtained upon purchase. (e.g. CS:GO loot box showing that it could contain one of X items upon purchase).

Important note: If a user removes an unsupportable mystery box from their offerings  or if they don't accept online payments for it, they are supportable.  
Pearl openings or pearl opening parties - the host opens oysters that the customer had  purchased. If an oyster contains a pearl, the customer receives it.

Government Products and Services 


Guideline 
Stripe does not support: 
Users that aim to mislead or scam consumers using misleading claims relating to government  products and services.
Government entities operating in foreign jurisdictions or government entities that want to use  Stripe to disburse economic support.  
Third-parties offering government products and services where no added value or authorisation  can be confirmed. 

Why are Government Services risky? 
Government entities are listed on our financial partners restricted business practices list, and depending  on the market, can be considered to be high risk merchants. This applies most typically to government  entities located outside of their country’s jurisdiction, as they are considered to be foreign entities.  Processing payments for these foreign government entities may bring up cross-border acquiring issues  or economic sanctions issues.  

In cases where government services may be outsourced to third parties, it’s important that third parties  demonstrate that they are authorized by the relevant government to provide those government services.  Third parties that provide government services without authorization pose a credit risk to Stripe from  chargebacks.  

Additionally, this is an area where there is a high risk of scams. Businesses may impersonate  government entities or falsely pretend to work on behalf of government entities to defraud or deceive  consumers (E.g., Businesses may provide services with unclear or low value add, or misleading services  (such as guaranteed visas). These types of services pose a credit risk to Stripe. 

What are Government Products and Services? 
This policy category covers products and services that are provided by government entities or by third parties on behalf of government entities. In addition, this category covers third-parties that offer products  and services which are supplemental to the government products and services. For the purposes of this  policy, use the following definitions: 
Government products and services, can be offered directly by a government entity or by a third  party on behalf of the government. Their goal is to fulfill a public need. Examples include: 
Travel and immigration, such as payment for travel visa services, passport services,  civil registration, such as payment for birth, marriage, or death registration or  certificates.  
Planning permission, such as payment for building or construction planning  permissions and permits.  
Licenses and permits, such as payment for occupational licenses, fishing permits, or  parking permits.  
Road and transport, such as payment for congestion charges, road tolls, parking  charges. 
Environment and utilities, such as payment of garbage collection fees, water treatment  fees. 
Public housing, such as payments for social or public housing rent, repairs and  maintenance.  
Law, such as the government provided legal aid or assistance. 
Payment for entry to public museums, libraries, parks, and community centers 
Government entity: National, federal, regional, state, and local entities controlled, run or owned  by the government.
Third party: Entities which are not government entities (E.g., Corporations, NGOs, sole  proprietors, individuals, etc.). 
Domestic jurisdiction: Services provided in the same country as the government entity  represents (E.g., A Canadian government entity providing services in Canada). 
Foreign jurisdiction: Services provided in a different country than the government entity  represents (E.g., A Canadian government providing services in Germany). 
Value add: Additional features or improved use case in addition to the standard product or  service. (E.g., Expedited processing, consultancy or advice services, courier services,  documentation translation assistance). 

Subcategory 1: Government Entity Offering Services and/or Products in a Foreign Jurisdiction 
Users that are government entities that offer services and/or products in a foreign jurisdiction are not  supportable.  
Government entities offering services and/or products in a foreign jurisdiction typically are:  
Embassies: An embassy is a country’s base for its diplomatic mission in another nation, where  the ambassador conducts their business, and its country’s citizens living or visiting abroad can  go for government services including visas, birth registrations, passports, voting, and legal help.  Embassies are always located in the capital of another country, with a few exceptions. 
Consulates: A consulate is an office that focuses on providing government services for citizens  abroad. Consulates may also offer passport or visa applications. 

Not Supportable
Regional Summary 
This subcategory is not supportable in all jurisdictions.

Hazardous Materials 

Guideline 
Stripe does not support the sale of hazardous materials, fireworks and non-mailable products.

Why are Hazardous Materials, Fireworks, and Non-Mailable Products risky?
Hazardous materials, as designated by the respective authorities, are either heavily regulated or their sale is outright illegal. This is why Stripe considers these not supportable. While there are legitimate ways businesses can sell some hazardous materials, Stripe currently does not have the appropriate insights and resources to safely enable any of them.    
Non-mailable products, as defined by USPS, are not supportable as they either pose danger of harm or they require dedicated forms of transport outside of USPS that Stripe is not currently able to verify.
What are Hazardous Materials, Fireworks, and Non-Mailable Products?
A hazardous material is any item or agent (biological, chemical, radiological, and / or physical), which has the potential to cause harm to humans, animals, or the environment, either by itself or through interaction with other factors. Designation of what is hazardous is governed by governmental agencies that supervise the respective type of material (e.g., EPA, OSHA, DOT, NRC). 
Non-mailable products are those that are included on US Postal Service (USPS) non-mailable lists since they are either hazardous or live animals that can be harmed during improper forms of transport. 
Fireworks are defined as devices for producing a visual or audio effect by the combustion of explosive or flammable compositions.

These types of services can be exaggerated or unnecessary. No company can fully protect you from  identity theft, they can only offer monitoring or recovery services. What are Identity Theft Protection and Monitoring services? 
Identity Theft Protection and Monitoring Services generally fall under either monitoring services (services  that watch for signs that an identity or credit thief may be using your personal information) or recovery  services (steps to take after credit or identity theft has occurred. Identity monitoring alerts you when your personal information (like your bank account  information or Social Security, driver’s license, passport, or medical ID number) is being used in  ways that generally don’t show up on your credit report. For example, these services might alert  you when your information shows up in change of address requests or court or arrest records,  or payday loan applications.

CBD: Acronym for cannabidiolum. A non-psychoactive compound found in cannabis.  
THC: Acronym for tetrahydrocannabinol, a psychoactive compound found in cannabis, which can create  an altered state of mind or a “high”

Mortgage Consulting 
Guideline 
Stripe places restrictions on mortgage consulting services. Stripe prohibits mortgage counseling,  mortgage relief, or mortgage reduction services if they appear predatory in nature or exhibit behaviors  indicative of mortgage consulting scams. Stripe may support mortgage consulting services that are  legitimate, are US Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) approved, and follow all legal  or regulatory requirements. 

Why is this category risky? 
The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) cites the Mortgage Assistance Relief Services (MARS) rule which  makes it illegal for any services offering mortgage relief or reduction to collect payment or fees from  homeowners before the homeowner has received and accepted an offer of relief from a lender. 

Mortgage relief/reduction is subject to many factors that typically cannot be changed or impacted by a  third party, including banks, hedge funds, secondary debt holders, credit rating, and credit history.  Mortgage consultants' may claim to protect consumers from home foreclosure and increased mortgage  payments, however liens are often exaggerated and predatory , on distressed consumers. These  businesses often charge consumers thousands of dollars up front but then deliver little or no aid and  possibly worsen the consumer’s debt. 

Our financial partners have cited this category on their unacceptable business practices list. Due to the  predatory nature of the business and the MARS rule in place to protect homeowners, Stripe carries a risk  of chargebacks and refunds if the promises made by the services are not fulfilled, as well as the legal risk  of such businesses collecting payment before they are legally allowed to do so.

Negative Option Marketing 

Guideline 
Stripe prohibits predatory and deceptive negative option marketing. Stripe restricts merchants offering  trial periods, as our financial partners similarly consider this billing model to be negative option marketing. 

Why is Negative Option Marketing risky? 
Merchants utilizing negative option marketing tactics intentionally deceive unsuspecting customers into  purchasing subscription packages, or products at higher prices than is readily apparent. True negative  option marketing behavior is unfair and deceptive, and has resulted in regulatory action by agencies such  as the FTC. It also leads to refunds and can be damaging to Stripe's reputation.  

What is Negative Option Marketing? 
This business model uses tactics to confuse and/or deceive their customer about the true cost and/or  duration of the product and/or service purchased. A frequent tactic is to automatically enroll customers  into a subscription or membership program without their knowledge or consent. This business model may  also enroll a customer in a subscription or membership program unless they take explicit action to opt-out  during the checkout/other stages of the transaction. without giving them a change of consent in an  informed way. It is often difficult to cancel such subscriptions/programs.  

This is in contrast to standard subscription, where a customer knowingly enrolls into a program under  clear conditions and with a reasonably easy-to-achieve cancellation process. 

Subcategory 1: Negative Option Membership Clubs 
Business models which advertise the sale of one instance of a product or a service (digital or physical), but subsequently enroll customers into a recurring auto-ship/auto-bill program or membership club upon purchase is not supportable on Stripe. 

Both of the criteria below have to be met for user to be not supportable:  
The User attempts to confuse the user by not using words like “subscription”, “recurring”,  “membership plan” or other terms indicative of a recurring shipping & billing in the product and  or service description or when selecting a delivery method.  
The User relies on a “fine print” method that discloses the recurring shipping and/or billing  nature of the product and/or service through a checkbox at the checkout after they have been  placed into a “cart” or only in Terms & Conditions. 

Additional signals that suggest user is likely to conduct this abuse:  
The User offers a “trial period” before signing customers up for a recurring shipment & user has  to proactively cancel to not be charged.  
There are pre-checked opt-ins during the checkout experience  

Important note: Having a checkbox offering customers to subscribe to a newsletter is not an  indicator of a negative option membership club. 

Not Supportable
Regional Summary 
This subcategory is not supportable in all jurisdictions.

User sells physical goods or recurring shipment of physical goods

The full price of the product is unclear (e.g., hidden in fine print during checkout in small, hard to read letters)

Sub-Category 3: Shop or Skip Plans 
The User offers a recurring shipment of a physical product on a monthly “shop or skip” basis and in order  for the customer to opt out, they have to do so within a limited time period (usually first few days of a  month).

Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs)

Guideline 
Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) are restricted on Stripe. 

Why is this category risky? 
NFTs are restricted by our financial partners as they pose risk for money laundering due to how  transactions can hide identities. As a novel type of a digital asset, NFT has a variable level of regulation  attached to it which also increases the risk profile of users that focus on them
 
What are NFTs? 
A NFT is a non-interchangeable unit of data stored on a blockchain, a form of digital ledger, that can be  sold and traded. This data can represent both real and virtual world items. Unlike cryptocurrencies, they  cannot be traded or exchanged at equivalency. 
NFTs are unique digital files commonly represented in the form of photos, videos, audio, as well as other  types of digital files or real-world items like artwork and property rights. 

Due to this space rapidly evolving, terminology still in flux. For the purposes of this policy and its  application, the following definitions are observed:  
NFT Minting: Process of NFT creation, predominantly on a blockchain. In most cases, the  minting party (“minter”) owns a cryptocurrency token that is utilized for the underlying blockchain  infrastructure. During the process of the minting, the properties of the NFT are determined.  
NFT Wallet: This is a storage solution that supports the blockchain protocol the NFT is built on.  For example if a NFT used Ethereum blockchain, it can be stored on an Ethereum wallet. An  NFT wallet can be custodial (i.e. the wallet provider holds the private keys to the wallet) or non custodial (where the user of the wallet holds the private keys to the wallet).  
NFT seller: The Stripe customer who is facilitating the sale, purchase or trade of NFTs. Stripe  supports users that are engaged in first-party sales, secondary sales (limited to closed-loop  transactions where the NFTs being resold were originally minted on the same site), or act as a  platform or all of these at once. Stripe does not support open loop secondary sales, defined as  the resale of a NFT that was originally minted on a site different than the one it is being resold on. 

What does this policy not cover?  
Cryptocurrencies or cryptocurrency exchanges must be reviewed under the Cryptocurrency products and  services policy.  

NFTs that are considered regulated securities (NFTs that help individuals and organizations store, move,  and invest money) must be reviewed under the Financial Services policy) as well as complying with the  NFT policy. 

Businesses or products that do not facilitate the actual transfer or sales of NFTs. These businesses are  supportable and include:  
Businesses selling information, tips, or forecasts about blockchain and/or NFTs.  
Business providing consultation for how to use blockchain and/or NFTs. 
Businesses facilitate the sale, purchase and trade of unique digital file in the form of photos,  videos, audio, and other types of digital files not represented by a NFT.  
Contracts “signed” via blockchain. 

Subcategory 1: First-Party NFT Sales  
A first party NFT sale is a user who is: 
The creator and/or minter and/or first seller of the NFT and offers to sell such NFT to their  customers directly,  
A marketplace that enables third parties to mint or sell NFTs 
A SaaS platform that offers services and infrastructure to NFT users that allow first-party sales.
Such user is supportable under the “Non-fungible token sales” policy, if: 
The sale, purchase or trade of NFTs does not violate Stripe's Adult Content, Products and  Services policy, Violent Extremism Policy, Unauthorized Use of Intellectual Property and  Counterfeit Goods policy or any other section of Stripe's Restricted and Prohibited Business List. 
The secondary sales of NFTs is not facilitated.

Prohibited Sports Performance Enhancing Chemicals 

Guideline 
Stripe does not support the sale of illegitimate sports performance-enhancing chemicals that are sold for  human consumption, sold without a specific purpose, and sold, but not used for research only purposes.  

Why are sports performance-enhancing chemicals risky? 
Some chemicals used in medical or other research can have an ambiguous legal status and present  significant regulatory risk from agencies such as the Drug Enforcement Agency (DEA) and Food and  Drug Administration (FDA). In addition, these chemicals can be abused to enhance sports performance  as defined by the World anti-doping agency (WADA) and can cause real-world harm to people. Due to  these factors, our financial partners restrict or prohibit their sale unless it is for research only purposes. 

What are sports performance-enhancing chemicals? 
Chemicals on the WADA prohibited list that are not covered by other policies - Illegal drugs and related  products , Marijuana and related businesses, and Healthcare products and services, and for which any of  the following are true, are considered prohibited sports performance-enhancing chemicals:  
They are sold for human consumption 
They are sold without a specific purpose 
They are sold, but not used for research only purposes 

Chemicals on the WADA prohibited list that are sold in its prescription-only form under a drug name or  other prescription-only chemical compound should be treated as a prescription pharmaceutical, refer to  the Healthcare products and services policy.  

The subcategories below focus on chemicals that are not, as of 2023, sold in the form of a prescription only drug.  

How to determine that the the prohibited sports performance-enhancing chemicals are sold for  human use:  
If there is no indication whether the chemicals are for human consumption, research only, or for  animal-use only, then prohibited chemicals have to be considered as “sold for human use /consumption” 
If the prohibited chemicals are not explicitly marketed and described as to be used for veterinary  or research purposes only, they have to be considered as “sold for human use/consumption”.
If there is any reference to sports or sports performance (E.g., Bodybuilding) on the user's  website despite the prohibited chemicals being explicitly marketed and described as for  veterinary or research purposes only, they have to be considered as “sold for human use /consumption”  
If the prohibited chemicals are explicitly marketed and described as for veterinary or research  purposes only, but they are sold alongside products (E.g., supplements) for humans, they have  to be considered as “sold for human use/consumption”  

Important note: If the prohibited chemicals are marketed as for veterinary use only, but they are  prescription-only veterinary drugs, refer to the Healthcare products and services policy.  

How to determine that the prohibited chemicals are sold, but are not used for research only  purposes: 
Stripe prohibits sale of any chemicals on the WADA prohibited list that are sold with the explicit or implicit  intent of being used to enhance a human's sports performance. This is typically in the context of body building, body-hacking, athletic performance improvement and other scenarios in which people seek to  use various supplements to improve their sports performance. 

Factors to determine that the user is selling prohibited chemicals, and they are not being used for  research only purposes: 
If users explicitly markets WADA prohibited chemicals as food supplements that can be  ingested, injected or otherwise consumed. The user would typically accompany the product with  a specific claim relating to its sport performance-enhancing effects, like that it “promotes muscle  growth” or that “athletes use it to promote muscle growth”. Such users are not considered to sell  the prohibited chemicals for research purposes only. 
If users sell WADA prohibited chemicals that can be ingested, injected or otherwise consumed  by humans without sufficient indicators confirming the user is selling them for research purposes  only. The following indicators may confirm that the user is not selling the prohibited chemicals  for research purposes only:  
There is little or no description of the products sold. Legitimate research chemical  sellers tend to be very descriptive, inclusive of highly specific chemical properties and  intended purposes of the chemical. 
Narrow product inventory focusing only on the prohibited chemicals. Legitimate  research chemical sellers tend to offer a wide range of products for laboratory research.  
Website does not contain any broader references to the commercial activity of the user  (E.g., No marketing materials focusing on their scientific credentials, no B2B focused  information, vague contact information etc).  
Website does not reference any safety procedures, registrations, ISO or other certifications 
The user sells products in quantities intended for human consumption (E.g., Individual  vials, sets of vials, blister packs, glass or plastic food supplements containers). This is  in contrast with legitimate sellers of research chemicals that offer very specific volumes, that are usually very small (micrograms, microliters) or very large (liters).
The user sells fitness and sports goods and services alongside the prohibited chemicals.  

Factors to determine the user is selling prohibited chemicals, but they are being used for research only  purposes: 
If the user self-describes as offering research chemicals to professionals (E.g., Labs, medical  facilities, universities etc).  
If the user offers a detailed description of the products sold. This would typically include the  research purpose, description of the compound at molecular level, which test subjects the  chemical can be used on etc.  
If the user offers a wide selection of products.  
If the user is descriptive of various safety procedures, ISO or other certifications and other  professional credentials relevant to them or to the seller 
If the user sells the chemicals either in very small quantities (microliters, micrograms) or in large  quantities (liters).  
Example of legitimate websites: https://lab.honeywell.com/en, https://www.acrobiosystems.com/ 

Important note: During the review, it has to be confirmed the user sells the prohibited chemicals for  research purposes only. If the user offers no indicators that they sell the prohibited chemicals for  research purposes only, then it has to be treated as if the user was selling prohibited chemicals for  human use.

Shipping Brokers and Other Third-Party Logistics Providers
(High Risk - Not Enabled for Wix)

Why are third-party logistics providers risky?

This category poses legal risk as Stripe has only limited  visibility into what products are being shipped and through which countries these products pass through.  This is a concern due to sanctions and anti-money laundering reasons.  This is reflected by financial partner restrictions that do exist in some regions that Stripe operates in. In addition, there are credit risks associated with the industry. 

What are third-party logistics providers? 

Third-party logistics providers (3PLs) facilitate the movement of goods for businesses, offering expertise in transportation and logistics management. This policy focuses on 3 types of businesses due to the specific risks they present to Stripe and due to financial partner restrictions. 

Shipping brokers: Shipping brokers act as intermediaries between shipowners who move goods and merchants who need to ship goods. They are focused on maritime shipping. They do not take direct possession of the end-users goods

Freight Forwarders: These are users who make arrangements and assume responsibility for moving goods from the point of origin to their final destination. Freight forwarding can engage multiple forms of transportation modes (air, land,  and sea). They can offer a wide range of services like route planning, cargo space booking, packing and handling, customs management, storage and tracking. 

Non-vessel operating common carriers (NVOCCs): These businesses act as a common carrier but do not actually own the vessel doing the transport. Instead, they book large amounts of space on ships and lease containers. Then, they sell space to shippers in smaller amounts and are responsible for the goods while they are being shipped. NVOCCs can have direct possession of the end-user goods. In addition, they organize the logistics and paperwork for shipping the goods, without dealing with the storage, loading, or unloading of the goods themselves.


Subcategory 1: Shipping Brokers and Freight Forwarding Services and Non-Vessel Operating Common Carriers (NVOCCs)  
This subcategory includes
Shipping Brokers: Shipping brokers act as intermediaries between  shipowners who  move goods and merchants who need to ship goods. They are focused on maritime shipping
By definition, they do not take direct possession of the end-users goods. Users that do would be considered courier & transport services and are not restricted or prohibited in any way. 
Freight Forwarders: These are users focused on finding the most cost-effective methods to move goods from the point of origin to their final destination. Freight forwarding can combine multiple forms of transportation modes (air, land,  and sea). They can offer a wide range of services like route planning, cargo space booking, packing and handling, customs management, storage and tracking. 
This is in contrast with basic logistics companies that focus on execution of specific tasks like transportation or warehousing. 
Non-vessel operating common carriers (NVOCCs): These businesses book large amounts of space on ships and lease containers. Then, they sell space to shippers in smaller amounts. 
This activity is typically part of a larger portfolio of services offered by a company.

Guideline 
Telemarketing businesses are prohibited on Stripe. 

Why is Telemarketing risky? 
Telemarketing is often used to scam people, employ predatory tactics or be otherwise abusive. Due to  robocalls and other automated systems, it has further gained a negative reputation. What is Telemarketing? 
A plan, program, or campaign involving telephone calls, video calls, or text messages that aims to promote the sale  of goods or services, charitable contributions or other financially motivated activity to consumers. These  calls and text messages are typically unsolicited, and are carried out either by people or by automated  machines (robocalls, AI bots).

Unauthorized Use of Intellectual Property and Counterfeit  Goods 


Guidance
Our Restricted Businesses list prohibits the use of our services for any business that directly infringes or  facilitates the infringement of different forms of intellectual property (“IP), such as trademarks or copyrights. 

Why is it risky for us to support businesses that may infringe the IP  rights of others? 
Processing for an infringing business poses at least three types of risks for Stripe. The first is legal: IP  owners could attempt to hold us “contributorily” liable if one of our users is offering infringing products or  services. The second is a partnership (and monetary) risk: processing for infringing products and  services is against card network rules, and could result in card brand violations. And the third is macro /systemic: trademarks can support our mission of increasing the GDP of the internet by enabling  consumers to trust that the products and services they purchase online (including from Stripe users) are  genuine, fully functional, and safe (which is especially true for products like pharmaceuticals, auto /machine parts, household items, etc. where the potential danger from a faulty or deficient counterfeit  item could be significant). 
 

Subcategory 1: Users primarily focused on selling counterfeits  
These are users that are primarily focused on the sale of counterfeit physical goods that imitate, replicate  or invite confusion with a legitimate product. Based on the indicators described below, it is less likely that  they will be able to provide Stripe with evidence that the goods they sell are authorized or not counterfeit  OR that they will be able to continue their commercial activity after removing violating items.  

What are counterfeit physical goods?  
Physical goods described as replica, knock-off, imitation, clone, faux, fake, mirror image, or  similar terms when referring to a brand name.  
Physical goods that mimic, replicate or imitate branded products in an attempt to create  confusion and to pass them off as genuine products. The examples below are not exhaustive:  
Handbags, wallets, designer clothing, sneakers or watches of luxury brands. Common  examples include fake Nike Jordan sneakers, Balenciaga hoodies, Rolex watches or  Coach wallets.  
Counterfeit Apple products like airpods or iphones using Apple branding explicitly or  referring to in description. 

Additional Indicators:  
Strong indicators that goods sold are counterfeit goods (these are only indicators. Their  presence alone does not automatically imply user is violation this policy) 
Goods are described as replica, knock-off, imitation, clone, faux, fake, mirror image, or  similar terms when referring to a brand name  
Goods sold for lower than usual price (price discounted by more than 65% compared to  top results in an internet search) 
Goods presented via bad quality/low resolution product photos/videos 
Goods accompanied by a very limited product description  
Sold on a website without features like terms and conditions, privacy policy, refund  policy, no “about” section, no “contact” section 
Sold on website with broken features like broken links 
Sold along completely unrelated product categories (shoes listed together with household appliances or kitchen equipment)  
Sold on website that redirects its visitors to spammy/clickbait/porn sites upon interacting with the website 

How to determine if a user is likely focused on selling counterfeit physical goods?  
Descriptions of the website or the store explicitly call out that it specializes on replicas, knock offs, imitations, clones, faux, fakes, mirror images of known brands. OR 
At least 50% of all items/merchandise sold are suspected counterfeits or at least 10 products (if  there are too many to count to determine the 50%).  

What are not counterfeit physical goods?  
Vintage, secondhand, and consignment goods 
Resellers of in-demand, limited edition items like sneakerbots & other resellers of “hypebeast”  items (e.g. seller of limited edition of Nike shoes that does not demonstrate any of the signals  above, seller of limited release Supreme products etc.)  
Users offering repairs, refreshment or personalisation  
Sellers of refurbished products (e.g refurbished iPhones or AirPods)  
New and legitimate goods 

Commonly counterfeited brands and products (List is not exhaustive): Chanel, Nike, Gucci, Rolex, Prada,  Louis Vuitton, Burberry, Cartier, Coach, Dior, Hermes, Tiffany, Yeezy, Armani, Yves Saint Laurent (YSL),  Bulgari, Celine, Chloe, Fendi, Givenchy, Balenciaga, Goyard

Unfair, Predatory, or Deceptive Practices 

Guideline 
Stripe will not support businesses that engage in acts or practices that are unfair, deceptive, or abusive acts or practices (UDAAP) to consumers. Such conduct, which is prohibited by federal and state law, is not limited to certain enumerated practices. Instead, these laws provide a general standard for users and an enforcement tool for courts and regulators to protect consumers from new and evolving types of harm.
Stripe can’t always detect a users UDAAP. But we must know the standard, stay up to date on conduct that regulators have characterized as UDAAP, and take appropriate action when a users UDAAP is identified. 

What is Unfair, Deceptive, or Abusive Acts or Practices (UDAAP)?
In the United States, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), and state attorneys general have the authority to enforce consumer protection laws that define and prohibit unfair, deceptive, or abusive acts or practices. The FTC has established precedent for interpreting these laws and other enforcers generally follow this precedent. With respect to abusive acts and practices, the CFPB has primary authority to enforce this relatively new and evolving standard in addition to the authority it shares with other enforcers.

What are Unfair, Predatory, or Deceptive Practices? This list is representative, but not exhaustive. 

Subcategory 1: Door to Door Sales  
Since “door to door sales” can often be made when the consumer is feeling under pressure to make a purchase, the FTC has regulations around sales they consider “door to door,” including a “cooling-off period” of three days in which the buyer has a right to cancel purchases over $25 USD without penalty. More information on FTC rules is available here.
Note: “door to door” sales can occur in the buyer’s own home, someone else’s home, a street corner, or in a place other than the seller’s normal place of business.
See the FTC’s Telemarketing Sales Rules for more information.

Subcategory 5: Products and Services that are Harmful, Deceptive, Unfair or Predatory

This subcategory includes any other business deemed by Stripe to be engaging in practices that are harmful, unfair, predatory, or deceptive to consumers or the wider public.
Deceptive or Predatory Products and Services:
A product or service that misleads or is likely to mislead the consumer; the consumer’s interpretation is reasonable under the circumstances; and the misleading representation, omission, act or practice is material. 
The reasonableness of a representation is determined based on how the target audience would interpret it. When there is more than one reasonable interpretation among the target audience and one is false, the representation is misleading. 
Examples:
Junk fees, hidden fees, including where users do not clearly disclose the initial charge, any subsequent recurring charges (late fees, interest rates, pricing etc), duration of any trial periods, frequency and duration of recurring charges, refund/cancellation policies, cross-sell/sell-up offers, duration of subscription or opt-out process, and negative billing options. Misrepresenting optional services or upgrades as mandatory, consumers buying tickets online or booking hotel rooms may find surprise junk fees included at checkout.
Bogus claims and endorsements, including where users employ sham product reviews (e.g. reviews posted from the same date or identical words used in several reviews) and comparison, high-pressure sales tactics with false/misleading promises regarding "limited-time", misleading or false promises on impact of product or service, fraudulent businesses (including fraudulent vaccines pr pseudo-pharmaceuticals), and users who are involved in transaction laundering (ex. fake charities who misrepresent their business model).
Unfair or Harmful Products and Services: 
A product or service is unfair if it causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers; cannot be reasonably avoided by consumers; and is not outweighed by countervailing benefits to consumers or to competition.
Substantial injury usually involves monetary harm, including, costs or fees paid by consumers as a result of an unfair practice. Forgone monetary benefits or denial of access to products or services may also cause substantial injury. An act or practice that causes a small amount of harm to a large number of people may be deemed to cause substantial injury. 
Consumers cannot reasonably avoid injury if the act or practice interferes with their ability to effectively make decisions or to take action to avoid injury. A key consideration is whether the consumer had a meaningful choice and could take action to prevent the injury. Users must not take action that interferes with consumers’ decision-making. 
Examples:
Users who share revenge porn or sexually explicit content without consent (E.x., Pick-up artists whose business model is to share content of “pursuits” without consent).
(Note: Services that offer non-harmful advice or information on relationships and dating, but that do not advocate practices that predatory or exploitative may be supportable) 
Users whose business models or products advocate for violence against or advocate self-harm (E.x., Suicide ideation, “pro-ana’ content ect). See Violent Extremism Policy for more information.
Users facilitating the creation of ‘deep-fake’ outputs (image, text, voice etc) using Artificial Intelligence models, with the intention of passing them off as ‘real’.
Medical products that have been the subject of criminal enforcement, injunctions (including from government officials), enforcement, seizures, or warning letters, (including FDA or FTC warning letters, search FDA database).
Medical devices that are damaged or misbranded. 
Used and refurbished subdermal medical devices (E.g., Acalpel, implants).
Users whose business models involve harassment of potential consumers (cold-calling) [see Door to Door Sales subcategory].
Abusive Products and Services:
An abusive product or service is one that takes advantage of a lack of understanding on the part of the consumer of the material risks, costs, or conditions of the product or service; the inability of the consumer to protect the interests of the consumer in selecting or using a consumer financial product or service; or the reasonable reliance by the consumer on a covered person to act in the interests of the consumer. An abusive user could also materially interferes with the ability of a consumer to understand a term or condition of a product or service.
A business that takes advantage of a lack of understanding on the part of the consumer of the material risks, costs, or conditions of the product or service.
A business interfering with the ability of a consumer to understand a term or condition of a product or service, including withholding or providing confusing information relevant to understanding the terms and conditions.
A business takes unreasonable advantage of gaps in the consumer’s understanding, unequal bargaining power, or reasonable reliance on the business. 
Examples:
Misleading claims are typically tied to highly desirable services and/or products provided by the government, where there is a high demand or where there is a high chance for unsuccessful application. Examples include: 
Services that offer a guaranteed outcome of the naturalization process

Services that offer a guaranteed outcome of a court process or promise a specific court date.

If in doubt, use the official online presence of the government entity to make the determination. 
Commonly impersonated agencies include IRS (US), Social Security Administration (US), Medicare (US), NHS (UK), HM Revenue & Customs (UK) and other. They tend to target older audiences.
Advertising prices that hide mandatory fees (“bait and switch” tactics) or misrepresenting the nature and purpose of fees.

Subcategory 10: Multi-Level-Marketing

Receive payment or other compensation that is based, in whole or in part, upon purchases, sales, or any other activities of the participant’s “downline.” (i.e., the collection of participants whom a participant has personally recruited (first level), any participants and customers recruited by first level participants (second level), any participants and customers recruited by second level participants (third level), and so forth, however denominated).
Participants may be brought into the business as contractors, independent business owners, distributors, or direct salespeople. These people are then tasked with selling the company's products and/or services to others, including family and friends. Sales can be done in person or online. An issue in determining the legitimacy of a multilevel marketing company is whether it sells its products or services primarily to consumers or to its members who must recruit new members to buy their products or services. Violent Extremism

Guidance
Stripe does not support users engaging in Unlawful Violence or Extremism as further defined below.

Overview of Stripe’s Policy on Violence and Extremism  
Stripe provides economic infrastructure to businesses of all kinds. We believe that, to the extent possible,  we should serve as an open, impartial provider of economic infrastructure. At the same time, in addition  to our legal obligations, we have responsibilities to our partners, our employees, and the communities in  which we live and operate. 

With all of this in mind, we have carefully scoped a policy definition and enforcement approach that  reflect our role as infrastructure and allow us to apply a principles-based standard that prohibits support  for users engaged in unlawful violence and extremism. If we do this well, we will continue to provide  services to some users that express views that some find objectionable or offensive, but will stop short of  doing business with users that engage in or perpetuate violence to others. 

The policy is: 
Informed by experts 
Globally applicable 
Capable of being applied consistently 
Reflective of the fact that we provide basic economic infrastructure -- we aren’t a content  platform, and shouldn’t moderate like one 
Reflective of our commitment to prevent the use of our services in ways that are likely to result  in unlawful violence and violent extremism 

REMINDER: Please refer to our Prohibited and Restricted Businesses page for the external articulation  of the policy. Everything in this document is internal only.  

What is in scope of the prohibition of our Unlawful Violence and Extremism policy? 
Any business, organization, or individual that predominantly engages in, promotes, or celebrates  unlawful violence or extremism. 

Sub Category 1: Unlawful Violence  
Unlawful violence is defined to include any business, organization, or individual that predominantly engages in, promotes, or celebrates unlawful violence or physical harm to persons or material damage to  property, including: 
Unlawful violent attacks that may cause injury or death to persons 
Unlawful and severe vandalism of property, including arson 
Clear displays of unlawful violent intent or threats 
Unlawful harassment that (a) targets an individual or a group of individuals and (b) threatens  serious physical harm to such individual(s)

Warranties 
Guideline 
Stripe prohibits extended warranties and restricts subscriptions over a year. 

Why are warranties risky? 
Extended warranties don’t always cover all repairs or maintenance. They can also make it difficult (via  fine print) to get the repairs when you actually need them. 

Our US financial partners have listed these services on their unacceptable business practices list. As  warranties cover an extended period, Stripe would carry the credit risk for the whole period, and so must  be considered carefully. 

What are warranties? 
Extended warranties are typically add ons for an additional cost that serve as insurance in case  something goes wrong with a purchase over an extended period of time. 

Subcategory 1: Standalone Extended Warranties Not Offered by the  Manufacturer  
Extended Warranties are add-on service contracts that offer repair and/or maintenance for a  specific period of time. They are added on to a purchase of a product and come at an additional  cost. 
Extended Warranties can also be called: 
Service Agreements
Service Contracts 
Maintenance Agreements
Service Plans 
Device Protection Plans

Weapons, Ammunition, and Related  Products 

Guideline 
Stripe prohibits the sale of weapons, ammunitions, and certain weapon components and accessories.  Users operating in this industry are subject to all applicable local and federal regulations, for both the  buyer’s and seller’s jurisdictions. 

Why is this category risky? 
Due to the impact weapons, ammunition, and certain related products can have on human life, this  category presents significant risks. These items are prohibited on Stripe due to the strict regulatory  requirements to ensure legitimate sales and distribution. 

What are weapons, ammunition, and related products? 
Weapons and ammunition are products that may inflict serious harm to others with intent or if misused.  The following subcategories represent common products and services in this space, but are not  exhaustive.

Subcategory 2: Non-functional Firearms 
Firearms that are incapable of firing a projectile:  
Antique firearms 
Replica antique firearms that resemble and are modeled on real firearms designed,  manufactured, and produced prior to 1898 (i.e. replica antique firearms) 
Replica firearms that resemble real firearms and were produced or manufactured and  entered into commerce after 5 May 1989, including devices modeled on real firearms  manufactured, designed, and produced since 1898, that has any of the following  markings or appearances: 
A blaze orange solid plug permanently affixed to the muzzle end of the barrel  as an integral part of the entire device and recessed no more than six  millimeters from the muzzle end of the barrel  
A blaze orange marking permanently affixed to the exterior surface of the  barrel, covering the circumference of the barrel from the muzzle end for a  depth of at least six millimeters. 
Construction of the device entirely of transparent or translucent materials  which permits unmistakable observation of the device's complete contents. 
Coloration of the entire exterior surface of the device in white, bright red,  bright orange, bright yellow, bright green, bright blue, bright pink, or bright  purple, either singly or as the predominant color in combination with other  colors in any pattern.
